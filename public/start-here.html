Beginner Path: build your first reliable systems.

A calm, step-by-step approach to self-sufficient living—start with the foundations that reduce stress and prevent common mistakes.

No perfection required—just one solid routine at a time.

Chickens: routine beats gear.

Focus on shelter, predator-proofing, feed/water consistency. Chickens thrive on predictable care routines more than expensive equipment.

Do this first

  • Coop sizing basics: 3-4 sq ft per bird inside, 8-10 sq ft per bird in run
  • Ventilation: add vents near roof to release moisture without drafts
  • Secure latches: use raccoon-proof locks on all doors and nest boxes
  • Simple daily check: walk the coop perimeter, refill water, collect eggs

Avoid these mistakes

  • Overcrowding: cramped birds stress easily and spread disease faster
  • Poor airflow: stale air leads to respiratory issues and ammonia buildup
  • Skipping nighttime security: predators hunt at night—lock up every evening

Water: safe storage and rotation.

Clean water is essential for survival. Establish a simple rotation system to keep your supply fresh and reliable.

Do this first

  • Choose food-safe containers: look for BPA-free plastic or stainless steel
  • Label fill dates: use permanent marker or tape to track freshness
  • Set rotation reminder: monthly calendar check to use oldest water first
  • Keep a basic filter backup: simple carbon filter for emergency purification

Avoid these mistakes

  • Unknown plastics: not all containers are safe for long-term water storage
  • No rotation plan: stagnant water loses quality and can harbor bacteria
  • Storing in direct heat: sunlight degrades plastic and encourages algae growth

Food preservation: start pantry-first.

Build your preservation skills gradually. Master dry goods and simple canning before moving to advanced techniques.

Do this first

  • Pick 3 staples: start with rice, beans, and flour—easy to store and rotate
  • Practice small batches: can a few jars of jam before tackling 20 quarts of tomatoes
  • Label everything: date, contents, and method on every jar or container
  • Store by use frequency: keep everyday items accessible, long-term stock in back

Avoid these mistakes

  • Too many methods at once: dehydrating, canning, fermenting—master one first
  • Unclear labeling: mystery jars waste food and create safety risks
  • Inconsistent storage conditions: temperature swings shorten shelf life dramatically
